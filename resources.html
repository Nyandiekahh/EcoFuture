Frequently Asked Questions

Climate change science is based on decades of research and data collected by thousands of scientists worldwide. The scientific consensus on human-caused climate change is supported by multiple lines of evidence including rising global temperatures, melting ice caps, rising sea levels, and increased frequency of extreme weather events. Organizations like the IPCC regularly review and synthesize this research to provide comprehensive assessments.

British Columbia faces several significant environmental challenges, including: forest management and old-growth protection, habitat loss and biodiversity decline, salmon population declines, water quality issues in some regions, climate change impacts such as more severe wildfires and flooding, and balancing resource development with conservation goals.

Individual actions that can make meaningful impacts include: reducing meat consumption (particularly beef), minimizing air travel, using public transportation or carpooling when possible, improving home energy efficiency, reducing consumption of new goods, supporting environmental policies and voting for candidates who prioritize climate action, and getting involved in community initiatives.

Starting a home compost is relatively simple. You'll need a bin or designated area, and you can compost most fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, eggshells, yard trimmings, and paper products. Avoid meat, dairy, oils, and pet waste. Maintain a balance of "greens" (food scraps, fresh yard waste) and "browns" (dried leaves, paper, cardboard). Turn your compost pile regularly and keep it slightly moist. For apartment dwellers, consider indoor worm composting or community compost programs.
